Corrosion & Cathodic Protection

Why metal rusts, how we stop it, and why zinc anode design actually matters.

What Is Corrosion?

Corrosion is a natural electrochemical process where metals break down over time. At its core, it’s just atoms losing electrons.

Simple idea: corrosion is metal slowly dissolving back into its natural state.

For iron or steel, this means:

  • Iron atoms lose electrons (oxidation)
  • They react with oxygen and water
  • This forms rust (iron oxide)

This isn’t just cosmetic damage. Rust weakens structures.

U.S. bridge corrosion costs $5.9–7.8 billion per year, and about 25–30% is preventable.

The Electrochemistry Behind It

Anode: where metal loses electrons

Cathode: where electrons are received

Oxidation: losing electrons

Reduction: gaining electrons

Corrosion works like a tiny battery forming on the metal surface.

Saltwater makes this worse because it acts as an electrolyte—a material that allows electricity (electron flow) to move easily.

Electrons flow from zinc → steel

How We Protect Against Corrosion

Detection

  • Fiber optic sensors
  • Chloride sensors
  • Moisture sensors

Prevention

  • Alloys (chromium, nickel, molybdenum)
  • Low-permeability concrete
  • Chemical inhibitors

Protection

  • Cathodic protection (GCP & ICCP)
  • Epoxy-coated rebar
  • Barrier coatings

Cathodic Protection Deep Dive

This method forces steel to act like a cathode so it doesn’t corrode.

Galvanic (GCP): A more reactive metal (like zinc) corrodes instead of steel.
Impressed Current (ICCP): External power pushes electrons onto the steel.

Used in real structures like:

  • Howard Frankland Bridge (FL)
  • Long Key Bridge (FL)
  • I-35W Saint Anthony Falls Bridge (MN)

The Research: Zinc Anode Size Matters

The key variable studied is surface-area-to-mass ratio (SA/M).

Higher SA/M → more surface exposed → more efficient electron flow
  • Smaller diameter zinc → higher SA/M → higher efficiency
  • But: smaller anodes wear out faster
Example:
  • 1 mm zinc ≈ ~90% mass loss
  • 3 mm zinc < 50% mass loss

Best result: 1.5 mm anode minimized rebar corrosion overall.

This shows a clear engineering tradeoff:

  • Too small → efficient but short lifespan
  • Too large → lasts longer but wastes material
Conclusion: “Bigger” is not better. Geometry and SA/M ratio matter.

Why This Matters

Florida infrastructure is especially vulnerable due to salt air and humidity.

  • Faster corrosion rates
  • Higher maintenance costs
  • Shorter structure lifespan

Optimizing zinc anode size could:

  • Reduce maintenance frequency
  • Lower material waste
  • Improve long-term durability
Future work: refine anode geometry and test long-term real-world performance.
